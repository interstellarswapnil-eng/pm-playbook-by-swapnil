---
title: Writing PRDs (that engineers will actually use)
---

## Goal
A PRD should reduce ambiguity and align the team on *what we’re building*, *why*, and *how we’ll know it worked*.

## A practical structure
1. **Problem** (1–2 paragraphs)
2. **Who it’s for** (personas / jobs-to-be-done)
3. **Success metrics** (inputs + outputs)
4. **In scope / out of scope**
5. **Requirements** (numbered, testable)
6. **User journeys** (happy path + edge cases)
7. **Non-goals & tradeoffs**
8. **Open questions**

## Common failure modes
- Requirements written as solutions (“Add a dropdown…”) instead of outcomes
- No explicit non-goals → scope creep
- No measurable success → bikeshedding

## Definition of done (PRD)
- A new engineer can explain the problem back to you
- Requirements can be turned into tickets without guesswork
- You can point to what’s explicitly *not* included
